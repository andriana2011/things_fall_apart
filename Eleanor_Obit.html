Obituary
Eleanor Nightingale

Journalist and writer at the heart of the story

Eric Sutherland
Friday September 30, 2000

Picture: Nightingale sharing a meal with members of the Women’s Liberation Front in Amurru, 1994. Despite public disinterest, she thought some stories were worth telling regardless. Photograph: From the Nightingale family archives.

My dear friend Eleanor Nightingale, who has died just shy of her 36th birthday, was a journalist, a twice-nominee for the Orwell Prize for Journalism, and a staunch believer that the power of stories is capable of great societal changes.

Born Ludston, to Amelia (née Knight) and Benjamin Nightingale, Eleanor attended Sutton Comprehensive, and then won a scholarship at Duality Church, Cambridge, to read history. During her studies, she had been a frequent contributor to Varsity, writing about disenfranchisement of poor students on campus, and women’s safety in the city. During her MPhil, she chaired The Society of Young Journalists.

Eleanor joined The Statesman (then Statesman and Society) in 1989, under the editorial leadership of Edward Weir. As she confessed to me years later: “It was a nest of blind and deaf bloodhounds – chasing the trail of some poor mangled pigeon while the Gallic Revolution was in full swing.” In the middle of – as it was later known by the public – the ‘Weir Affair’, Eleanor started a blog under the pseudonym Salem, where she covered the stories deemed by the senior editors to be of ‘no importance’. Her piece detailing the financial mismanagement, personal enrichment of Ludsten Mayor James Croft, and the complicity of The Statesman in destroying the evidence, earned Eleanor her first Orwell Prize nomination.

From 1993 to 1999, she wrote for The Guardian. Initially tasked with covering stories inside the Britannic Island, Eleanor found her true passion in war journalism. She sought to illuminate the conflicts the Western world was uncomfortable, where no man or woman had been brave enough to go: the Women’s Revolution in Amurru (1994-95), the attempted genocide in Mille-Collines (1995-1996), the Technocyte outbreak (1998-1999). She brought the reader directly with her into the heart of the issues, from the mixed-gender barracks of Rojava, to the abandoned metro lines in Holvania.

Eleanor’s reporting on the Technocyte Outbreak would earn her the second Orwell Prize in journalism nomination.

Eleanor (Rose) Nightingale, journalist, born 2 November 1965; died 17 September 2000. She is survived by her parents, Amelia and Benjamin, and her younger brother, Arthur. She is reunited with her fiancé Christopher beyond the veil.
